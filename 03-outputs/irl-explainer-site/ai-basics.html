AI Basics for Physicians

Developing Process Skills, Not Product Dependencies
Ernest Pedapati, MD

This is not a sales pitch for a product. There is no subscription to sign up for, no platform to commit to, and no app that will be obsolete in six months. What follows is a way of thinking about AI as a clinical and research tool, one that belongs to you the same way a stethoscope belongs to you. You learn how to use it. You develop judgment about when and how to apply it. The skill travels with you regardless of which technology you happen to be using.

The AI landscape changes fast. A product that dominates today may not exist next year. If your entire workflow depends on one company's interface, you start over every time the market shifts. But the physicians who will get the most out of AI are not the ones who memorize which buttons to click in a particular app. They are the ones who develop a repeatable process: formulate a clear question, give the AI structured instructions, review the output critically, and refine. That process works with any AI system, today or five years from now.

Think about how you learned to use a stethoscope. Nobody taught you "how to use the Littmann Cardiology IV." They taught you auscultation. The instrument is interchangeable. What matters is the skill: knowing where to listen, what to listen for, and what the findings mean in context. AI is the same. The model is the instrument. The skill is knowing how to direct it, evaluate its work, and integrate the results into your professional judgment.

1. App-Based AI vs. Professional AI Skills

Two ways to use AI. One makes you dependent on a product. The other makes you better at your work.

Most physicians encounter AI through apps: a chatbot with a friendly interface, a "summarize this" button in a note-taking tool, a pre-built template in a commercial platform. These can be useful. But they train you to rely on someone else's decisions about what AI should do and how it should do it. When the app changes its interface, raises its price, or disappears entirely, your "AI skills" go with it.

The alternative is to develop process skills that you own. You learn how to write clear instructions for any AI. You learn how to structure a task so the output is reviewable. You learn how to catch errors, request revisions, and build on previous work. These skills are yours. They work across every AI system and they compound over time.

2. What Process Skills Look Like in Practice

A repeatable method that works with any AI, for any task.

A process skill has a simple structure. You write down what you want the AI to do, in plain text. You specify your inputs, your assumptions, and what the output should look like. The AI reads your instructions and produces files: a summary, a draft, a data table. You review the output, note what needs to change, and run it again. Each round gets better because the instructions accumulate your reasoning.

This is not fundamentally different from how you work with a human research assistant or a medical student. You would not hand someone a stack of papers and say "do something useful." You would give them a specific question, point them to the relevant sources, and tell them what format you need the answer in. Then you would review their work and give feedback. AI works the same way. The difference is that AI never gets tired, never takes the feedback personally, and can reprocess the entire task from scratch in minutes.

The plan file is where your reasoning lives. It is a plain text document that any AI can read. No proprietary format, no vendor lock-in. If you switch from one AI to another, the plan file still works. Your instructions, your accumulated revisions, your quality checks: all of it travels with you.

3. Why This Matters for Physicians

Clinical judgment is not something you outsource. AI should amplify it, not replace it.

Physicians are trained to synthesize complex information under uncertainty. That is exactly the skill AI needs from you. An AI can search a database, summarize 50 papers, or draft a protocol. But it cannot decide whether the question is the right question. It cannot judge whether the summary missed the one finding that changes the clinical picture. It cannot notice that the protocol makes an assumption that does not hold for your patient population.

Your job is not to use AI. Your job is to direct it with the same rigor you bring to everything else in medicine. Write down your reasoning. Specify what you need. Review the output like you would review a resident's note. Push back when something is wrong. The AI improves because your instructions improve, not because the software got an update.

This approach also protects you. When your workflow is a documented series of instructions and reviewed outputs, you have a clear record of what was done and why. You can trace any result back to the reasoning that produced it. That matters for research reproducibility, for clinical documentation, and for your own confidence in the work.

4. Getting Started

You do not need to install anything to begin. You need a text file and access to any AI assistant.

Start with a single question you want AI to help you with. It could be a literature search, a data summary, a draft of a methods section. Write down what you want in a plain text file: what the input is, what assumptions you are making, and what the output should look like. Then give those instructions to an AI and review what comes back.

That is the entire method. Write instructions. Run them. Review the output. Revise the instructions based on what you learned. Each iteration makes the work better and your instructions sharper. Over time, you build a library of plan files that capture how you think about different types of problems. That library is your AI skill set, and it works with whatever AI tool you happen to be using.
